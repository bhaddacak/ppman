\chapter{Dictionaries}\label{chap:dict}

When you read Pāli/Sanskrit text, an indispensable tool is dictionaries. So, a good Pāli/Sanskrit text reader needs a good integration of good dictionaries. Unfortunately, we really have limited resources of Pāli dictionaries. All we have here, are all we can get in digital form. Most of them are rather old. A game changer recently is Digital Pāḷi Dictionary (DPD), see further Chapter \ref{chap:dpd}.

\section{Pāli dictionaries}

Pāli dictionaries supported by the program are listed below. The first five items are bundled with the program and ready to use.

\textbf{(1) Concise Pāli-English Dictionary (CPED).}\footnote{By Ven.\ A.\,P. Buddhadatta, available online at \url{www.budsas.org/ebud/dict-pe/index.htm}} This dictionary holds a significant position in the program. It is the only one that is structured and used widely in various contexts. I also made a few corrections and added some entries.

\textbf{(2) Concise English-Pāli Dictionary (CEPD).}\footnote{By Ven.\ A.\,P. Buddhadatta, available online at \url{www.budsas.org/ebud/dict-ep/index.htm}} This may be less used but has its own merit. It can give you a word that you do not know it in Pāli, but you know its close English terms. Although you can search in meanings of all dictionaries, simple searching in this dictionary is faster and sufficient in most cases.

\textbf{(3) New Concise Pāli-English Dictionary (NCPED).}\footnote{Compiled by SuttaCentral (\url{suttacentral.net}), updated and corrected from Margaret Cone's Dictionary of Pāli} Since this dictionary is derived from CPED, you can find redundancies here. But many entries are corrected and expanded. It is still concise and handy to use. Using this dictionary to recheck with CPED is a good idea. Unfortunately, Ven.\,Sujato told me that NCPED is now obsolete, no further update, and DPD is preferable to use instead.\footnote{Comparing the size and complexity of DPD and NCPED, I think NCPED still has its place in modern studies of Pāli. Sometimes, if not most of the time, I just need a simple search and just enough result, not mind-boggling details.}

\textbf{(4) The Pali Text Society's Pali-English Dictionary \\(PTSD).}\footnote{Originally this is written by T.\,W.\ Rhys\ Davids and W.\ Stede (1921--25). It is subsequently revised in accordance with the 2015 ``Reprint with corrections'' by K.\,R.\ Norman, William\ Pruitt and Peter\ Jackson. The digital version is released by \emph{Buddhadust} (\url{buddhadust.net}), proofread 2021. For the source used here, see \url{vpnry.github.io/ptsped}.} This a little outdated dictionary is still indispensable and often gives some insight to the words we study.

\textbf{(5) Dictionary of Pāli Proper Names (DPPN).}\footnote{By Gunapala Piyasena Malalasekera, available online at \url{www.palikanon.com/english/pali\_names/dic\_idx.html}} You may seldom use this one. It gives detailed information about Pāli names, not directly an explanation of the terms.

\textbf{(6) Mini Digital Pāḷi Dictionary (MDPD).} This is a minimal/optimal version of the big DPD. It will be created when you get the DPD and process it for the first time. This dictionary is much more concise than the full DPD and can be used with search functionality along with other dictionaries.

\textbf{(7) Margaret Cone's Dictionary of Pāli (CONE).}\footnote{Three volumes, 2001-2021, Pali Text Society, available online at \url{gandhari.org/dictionary}} This is the newest and authoritative Pāli dictionary in print. Its content is voluminous with a lot of examples, hence highly valuable for students. As far as I know, even though the dictionary can be searched online, its license is proprietary. That is the reason it has to be installed manually by the user.\footnote{Primarily, I added Cone's dictionary to use by myself only. Because of its usefulness to Pāli learners, I share the data file personally as an unofficial resource.}

Before you can use dictionaries 1--5 above, you have to create their data first because the program is only shipped with raw dict data in CSV format. These data have to be converted into tables in Dict database. Normally, creating dict data is a very first action you do when you successfully make the program run for a serious use. To do this, just select menu Dict > {\faSix \symbol{"F1C0}} Create Dict data, and wait for a while until the Dict engine is ready (the tab of Pāli Dictionaries is accessible).

\section{Sanskrit dictionaries}

Many Pāli learners know little about Sanskrit grammar. But looking up words in Sanskrit dictionaries requires just a little knowledge of grammar. Moreover, searching Sanskrit dictionaries can give them some insight about the words unavailable in any Pāli dictionary. That is the main reason I added Sanskrit dictionaries here. An additional thing Pāli learners have to know is the way Sanskrit words represented in Pāli. You may find that some terms are identical, some look similar, but some have to be learned. Read further in \emph{Conversion between Sanskrit and Pali forms}.\footnote{\url{en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pali}}

Sanskrit dictionaries selected here are listed as follows:

\textbf{(1) M. Monier-Williams' Sanskrit-English Dictionary (MW).}\footnote{Data files and information of all dictionaries can be found at Cologne Digital Sanskrit Lexicon (CDSL) project (\url{sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de}). Sanskrit words from here are encoded in SLP1. However, the 1899 edition of MW is also maintained at \url{github.com/sanskrit-lexicon/MWS}. I use its data file from here because it underwent certain improvements and it also has IAST version.} This one is the most comprehensive Sanskrit dictionary we have so far. This helps me a lot when I cannot find a viable meaning of some Pāli terms.

\textbf{(2) V.\,S. Apte's Practical Sanskrit-English Dictionary (AP).}\footnote{From revised edition, 1957--59} This is second to MW. The formation of words in the entries is different from MW. AP normally uses nominative singular forms for nominals, where as MW uses hypothetical stem-form. For example, AP has \pali{gajaḥ} (m.) and \pali{nagaram} (nt.), whereas MW has \pali{gaja} and \pali{nagara}, but both use \pali{latā} (f.).

\textbf{(3) Jīvānanda Vidyāsāgara Bhaṭṭācāryya's Śabda-sā\-gara (SHS).}\footnote{1900 edition} This dictionary may not be informative like the two above, but it provides short and clear definitions.

\textbf{(4) Arthur A. Macdonell's Sanskrit-English Dictionary (MD).}\footnote{1893 edition} This is more handy than the first two and has less words than SHS. You may find this useful sometimes, but it is overshadowed by the three above.

\textbf{(5) Franklin Edgerton's Buddhist Hybrid Sanskrit Dictionary (BHS).}\footnote{1953 edition} This is a special dictionary. It is useful when you read Buddhist Sanskrit texts. To Pāli learners, it can also give you interesting information in some matters.

\textbf{(6) M. Monier-Williams's English-Sanskrit Dictionary (MWE).}\footnote{1851 edition} The last three dictionaries are English to Sanskrit. They can give you a quick lookup for Sanskrit terms that you know only their English meaning. Among these, MWE has more words.

\textbf{(7) V.\,S. Apte's Student's English Sanskrit Dictionary (AE).}\footnote{1964 edition, the data file is taken from \url{github.com/sanskrit-lexicon/ApteES}}

\textbf{(8) Anundoram Borooah's English-Sanskrit Dictionary (BOR).}\footnote{1877--81}

Sanskrit dictionaries are not shipped with the program. The users have to download the data files before use, by menu Sanskrit > {\faSix \symbol{"F381}} Download Sanskrit dictionaries. The downloader is shown in Figure \ref{fig:sktdict-downloader}.\footnote{To understand the downloader, see Chapter \ref{chap:dpd} on the part of downloading.} You can select each dictionary separately. MW is big (around 50 MB). You may deselect this to download later. Or you can select only one of them for a quick test. Hit Start button when you are done with the selection. Once the download is finished, the SktDict database is created and the tool is ready to use. If you want to recreate the database in case you have updated some dict data files manually, use menu Sanskrit > {\faSix \symbol{"F1C0}} Create Skt.\ Dict data. Sanskrit Dict DB can be locked and unlocked like Pāli Dict DB described in the box above.

\section{Using Pāli dictionaries}

When Dict data are available, you can open a Dict window (or use Pāli/Sanskrit Dict tabs) and enter a search query. In this section we will focus on Pāli first. To open a Pāli Dict window, see the instruction box below.

\dothis{To open Pāli Dictionaries, press {\faSix \symbol{"F02D}} button in the main tool bar, or select this menu:\\
\menucommand{Dict > {\faSix \symbol{"F02D}} Dictionaries}
}

On the top, there are checkboxes of each dictionary to be included in a search. The default selection can be set as described in the previous section. You can select or deselect a dictionary needed at any time, if it is available.

By default, when you enter a query in the search field\footnote{Now the query can be in any of supported scripts.}, you have to hit Enter or press Search button to start the search. This behavior can be changed to incremental search when this option is checked. By this, once you enter the first letter, it starts the search right away. This can take time because a lot of terms have to be retrieved. When a subsequent letter is entered, the result will be narrowed down quickly. Incremental search is not suitable for MDPD because its massive results can slow down the operation considerably.

\boxcaveat{Use incremental search when a few dictionaries are included, and do not use with MDPD.}

An example of a search can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:pali-dictionaries}.

\section{Dictionary search modes}

Searching in dictionaries can be one of the following three use cases:

\subsection*{1. Simple search}

Simple search is fast and simple. It can be incremental if this option is selected (see a caution above). You enter some characters, relevant results started with the query will be listed, and the topmost entry is shown in the result area. Figure \ref{fig:pali-dictionaries} shows a result of simple search. This mode of searching is used most of the time.

\subsection*{2. Wildcard search}

Sometimes you have a vague idea what the word looks like. You can use wildcard mode by enabling \textbf{Use */?}. In this mode, you can use ? standing for any one character, and * standing for any characters (including none). Figure \ref{fig:dictionaries-wildcards} shows the result of `*mutt?'. Remember that in wildcard mode you always have to press Search button or hit Enter.

\begin{figure}[!hbt]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{images/dictionaries-wildcards.png}\\
\caption{Wildcard search in Pāli dictionaries}
\label{fig:dictionaries-wildcards}
\end{figure}

As shown in the picture, * can mean zero character, and ? always represents one character. So, we also see \pali{mutta, muttā,} and \pali{mutti} in the result list. In the result of CPED, if the selected term is declinable (either noun or adj.), we will see \fbox{Show declension}. When this box-button is clicked, a declension window of that term will be opened.

\subsection*{3. Meaning search}

Yet sometimes you have no clue what the word should be. If looking up in English-Pāli/Sanskrit barely helps, searching in the meanings of dictionaries can help. Figure \ref{fig:dictionaries-meaning} shows the result when searching `emancipate.' In this mode, using wildcards is not allowed, and do not forget to press Search button or hit Enter to submit the query. When a result is shown, only the first occurrence of the searched word is highlighted.

\begin{figure}[!hbt]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{images/dictionaries-meaning.png}\\
\caption{Meaning search in dictionaries}
\label{fig:dictionaries-meaning}
\end{figure}

You can search further in the result with finding tool by press \texttt{Ctrl-F} and use its navigator to see the next or previous occurrences (see Figure \ref{fig:dictionaries-find-in-result}), or simply press \texttt{Ctrl-G} and \texttt{Ctrl-Shift-G}.
